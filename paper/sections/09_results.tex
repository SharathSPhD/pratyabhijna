\subsection{H1.v4--H4.v4: per-domain cascade vs.\ bare}\label{sec:results_h1_h4}

The four primary contrasts (one per domain) are inconclusive at the v0.4 pilot $n$. H1.v4 (\texttt{aut}, $n=5$) is directionally negative ($g=-0.32$; BCa CI on paired mean $\Delta$ $[-0.06,\,+0.01]$): on the alternative-uses task, the cascade did not improve over bare Haiku, and at this small $n$ the BCa CI is consistent with a small negative paired mean delta. H2.v4 (\texttt{poetry\_interp}, $n=10$) is the largest positive effect ($g=+0.32$; BCa CI on paired mean $\Delta$ $[-0.014,\,+0.146]$) but the upper bound brushes the threshold rather than crossing it; the Wittgenstein aspect-shift items are where the cascade shows its best signal but the pilot $n$ is too small to make the BCa CI on paired mean $\Delta$ strictly positive. H3.v4 (\texttt{poetry\_gen}, $n=6$) is essentially null ($g=+0.14$, CI $[-0.05,\,+0.06]$), and H4.v4 (\texttt{sci\_creativity}, $n=4$) is positive ($g=+0.30$) with a CI $[-0.013,\,+0.061]$ that includes zero. Length-controlled effects are smaller in absolute value across all four domains, suggesting the cascade's slightly longer surfaces (a function of the second-pass revision) carry a small fraction of the un-controlled signal. None of H1.v4--H4.v4 is supported under the pre-registered ``Holm-adjusted $p<0.05$ \emph{and} BCa CI strictly positive'' criterion. We discharge the negative-result obligation: the v0.4 pilot does not show a positive cascade-vs-bare effect at the pilot's available sample.

The retrospective power numbers are instructive about why we are inconclusive rather than convincingly null: H1.v4 retrospective power is 0.012, H2.v4 is 0.235, H3.v4 is 0.090, H4.v4 is 0.122. At an a-priori target $g=0.5$ the per-domain $n$ would have to roughly triple to get the per-domain power above 0.8. The v0.5 ladder targets exactly this scale-up; it is the principal driver of the v0.5 budget request. Figure~\ref{fig:v04power_vs_realised} renders the trade-off visually.

See Figure~\ref{fig:v04h5} for a forest plot of H1--H4 alongside the H5.v4 fixed-effects pool. Per-axis paired effects on the same four contrasts are reported in Table~\ref{tab:per_axis_effects} and Figure~\ref{fig:v04axes}; the table makes it clear that within each domain a single axis carries most of the directional signal (\texttt{aspect\_count} for H2, \texttt{imagery} and \texttt{emotional\_resonance} for H3, \texttt{frame\_coverage} for H4), with the remaining axes flat or against the architecture's hypothesis.

\subsection{H5.v4: fixed-effects meta-pool}\label{sec:results_h5}

H5.v4 (re-registered per ADR-005 from the v0.3 random-effects pool to a fixed-effects pool, on the grounds that four studies is too few to estimate $\tau^2$ reliably) reports a pooled $g={V04\_H5\_POOLED\_G}$, fixed-effects 95\% Wald CI on pooled $g$ $[{V04\_H5\_CI\_LO},\,{V04\_H5\_CI\_HI}]$, with inverse-variance weights $0.20,\,0.40,\,0.25,\,0.16$ for H1.v4,H2.v4,H3.v4,H4.v4 respectively. The pool is not strictly positive (the CI crosses zero) and is therefore not supported under the pre-registered criterion. The largest contributing weight is H2.v4 (\texttt{poetry\_interp}); if the pilot had run at twice the per-domain $n$, the H5.v4 CI would have been roughly half its width and the pool would be approaching but not crossing the zero line at the current effect estimates. We do not change the criterion post hoc.

\subsection{H8a.v4: shadow-revision quality is robust}\label{sec:results_h8a}

H8a.v4 is the load-bearing positive finding of the v0.4 pilot. Within \texttt{haiku\_cascade\_always\_revise}, the paired \texttt{score(surface\_revision)} $-$ \texttt{score(surface\_draft)} is positive on $n=27$ items: paired mean $\Delta = +0.058$, Hedges' $g = +0.65$, BCa 95\% CI on paired mean $\Delta$ $[+0.031,\,+0.095]$, paired permutation $p = {V04\_H8A\_P}$ (one-sided). The CI is comfortably positive, the $g$ is in the medium-to-large range, and the permutation $p$ is below any reasonable $\alpha$. We read this as: \emph{the cascade reliably produces a better surface in the second pass}. What it does not yet do reliably is \emph{commit} the better surface; H8b and H8c are about that gap.

\subsection{H8b.v4 and H8c.v4: gate calibration and commit-policy comparison}\label{sec:results_h8bc}

\paragraph{H8b: gate calibration.} Treating the binary task ``predict that the revision is better than the draft'' as a classification problem, the event-gated commit policy reports F1 = 0.516, accuracy = 0.444, precision = 1.0, recall = 0.348 (over $n=27$, supports 23 positive / 4 negative). The learned-gate commit policy reports F1 = 0.647, accuracy = 0.556, precision = 1.0, recall = 0.478 over the same items. Both policies are perfectly precise (when they fire, they fire on cases where the revision is in fact better) but both have low recall (they decline to fire on a substantial fraction of the cases where they should). The learned gate's higher recall is the principal driver of its higher F1; the architectural takeaway is that a logistic head trained on the H8a paired-revision ground truth materially improves over a hand-coded \iast{vimar\'sa} disjunctive event signature.

\paragraph{H8c: commit-policy leaderboard.} The four commit policies, ranked by paired mean $\Delta$ versus \texttt{haiku\_bare} on the common $n=25$ paired set, run as follows. \texttt{always\_revise} sits at the top with $\Delta = +0.051$, $g=+0.53$, BCa~95\% CI on paired mean $\Delta$ $[+0.022,+0.096]$, and a paired-permutation $p=0.0028$. \texttt{learned\_gate} follows at $\Delta = +0.039$, $g=+0.39$, BCa~95\% CI on paired mean $\Delta$ $[+0.008,+0.087]$, $p=0.026$. \texttt{event\_gated} comes in at $\Delta = +0.020$, $g=+0.21$, BCa~95\% CI on paired mean $\Delta$ $[-0.008,+0.066]$, $p=0.158$ (i.e.\ inconclusive at $\alpha=0.05$). \texttt{always\_draft}, the degenerate baseline, sits at $\Delta = -0.007$, $g=-0.07$, BCa~95\% CI on paired mean $\Delta$ $[-0.039,+0.043]$, $p=0.635$. Pairwise paired-permutation tests reject \texttt{always\_revise}${}={}$\texttt{learned\_gate} ($p\approx 0.034$) and \texttt{event\_gated}${}={}$\texttt{always\_draft} ($p\approx 0.0073$); the other four pairwise comparisons are not significant at $\alpha=0.05$. The headline reading is therefore \texttt{always\_revise}${}>{}$\texttt{learned\_gate}${}>{}$\texttt{event\_gated}${}>{}$\texttt{always\_draft}, with the gap between the top two and the bottom two being where the cascade earns its keep. The learned gate would, in principle, be the cost-effective choice --- it would skip the second pass on items it predicts will not benefit, halving the per-item Haiku spend on those items --- but its recall is still well below the unconditional always-revise commit, so the cost-quality trade-off is what the v0.5 ladder targets.

\subsection{H9.v4: judge-vs-scorer agreement is essentially zero}\label{sec:results_h9}

H9.v4 measures the per-item agreement between two independent evaluators of the cascade-vs-bare contrast: the Sonnet-4 judge and the Haiku composite-score head used inside the cascade. On the $n=23$ items where both scores are available, Spearman $\rho = 0.0$ ($p = 1.0$), and the sign-agreement rate (do the judge and the scorer agree on the sign of the cascade-vs-bare delta?) is 0.565. The judge and the scorer are not measuring the same thing on the cascade items at this scale. The H9 result is not supported as a positive correlation, but it is a load-bearing finding: any cascade architecture that uses a small-LM scorer to gate a large-LM commit must reckon with the possibility that the gate is firing on signal that is uncorrelated with what the downstream judge actually rewards. \S\ref{sec:discussion} interprets this against the LLM-as-judge literature \citep{ZhengEtAl2023JudgingLLMAsJudge,LiuEtAl2023GEval,Braun2025AcquiescenceBiasLLMs,LauritoEtAl2025PNASAIAIBias}.

\subsection{Cost ledger}\label{sec:results_cost}

The v0.4 cost ledger separates the Haiku cascade and the Sonnet judge, because they are different models running different prompts and conflating them was an artefact of an earlier roll-up. The Haiku cascade arms (\texttt{haiku\_bare}, \texttt{haiku\_cascade}, \texttt{haiku\_bare\_2K\_scorer}, \texttt{haiku\_generic\_revise\_2pass}) consumed $\$12.73$ over $1{,}277$ managed-API calls, recorded in \texttt{audit/v0.4/cost\_ledger\_merged.json}. The stratified Sonnet judge added $\$0.48$ over 23 paired judgements, recorded in the audit block of \texttt{benchmarks/results\_v0.4/judge\_agreement.json}. The combined v0.4 pilot spend was therefore $\$13.21$ over $1{,}300$ CLI invocations, and ran for roughly 200 minutes of wall-clock time across four parallel domain runs. Per-domain Haiku-cascade spend ranges from $\$2.71$ (\texttt{poetry\_interp}, 494 calls --- the largest domain) to $\$3.48$ (\texttt{poetry\_gen}, 296 calls). The cost-per-pair is dominated by the cascade's $K{=}3$ candidates plus the always-shadow revision; reducing $K$ to 2 would roughly cut the cascade arms' spend by a third. Figure~\ref{fig:v04cost} reports the per-domain Haiku-cascade breakdown; the Sonnet judge surcharge does not enter that figure because the judge runs out-of-band on a 23-pair stratified subset. Table~\ref{tab:cost_split} reports the same numbers split per model line.
